The monumental city





Pão d´Assucar , 1890

If parks and gardens 'landscaped' Santiago and Buenos Aires into cities that showcased the 'domesticated nature' which was, in many ways, the very image of the nation cherished by the agro-exporting elites, Rio de Janeiro with its impressive skyline of mountains and bays had been a theme of American iconography since the earliest days of the picturesque. Along with other geographical features and buildings and monuments, the urban landscape integrated, on catalogues and maps produced for display at universal exhibitions, a 'system of sights' that provided a vision of Brazil 'at a glance' for the visitor from overseas, carefully cleansed of undesired and 'uncivilised' presences.
