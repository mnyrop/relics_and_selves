The African other



  A. D. Bressac, Allegory on the Law of the Free Womb

Representations of slavery in the nineteenth century are more common among European traveling artists, often depicting the harshness of living conditions and punishments endured by slaves, than among Brazilians. In fact, much of Brazilian art during the Imperial era studiously avoided the issue in favour of historical or literary subject matter related to the earlier colonial period and to fantasies about chivalric indigenous warriors and seductive vixens of the forest. Where the issue appeared at all in official art, in was often in a highly allegorical fashion, and in praise of the Imperial regime's policy of moderate, gradual advances towards liberation, as in the above statue made in homage to the law of 1871 that granted free status to all children born from enslaved parents (though not to the latter).
